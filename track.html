The Velodrome

The track itself - The velodrome

The track, called a velodrome, is a purpose-built bicycle racing arena. Modern velodromes have standardized measurements, consisting of two 180-degree bends connected my two straghts. The bends are steeply banked, but transition into the not so steep straights.

Why is it banked?

So you can go faster! With the curves of the track being banked, you don't have to slow down!

Track markings - What do they mean?

The velodrome featurs multiple colored lines that go all the way around the track. These lines are usually laid out in a specific way.Between the infield and the track itself lies the blue "cote d'azur".This is not a part of the track, and riding on it to overtake a competitor will disqualify you. 20 cm above the "cote d'azur" lies the black measurment line, which defines the track length. If Olympic or World Championship events are to be held at a velodrome, the measuring line, and thus also the track itself, must be the standard 250 m. The black line also indicates the optimal route for a track cyclist to take around the track, as it would be the shortest. 5 cm above the measuring line is the red sprinter's line. Between the sprinter's line and the measuring line is the sprinters lane. A rider in the sprinter's lane may not be passed on the inside. At the middle of the track's width is the blue stayer's line. This line is used in races in which motorbikes are used for pacing. During such events, the motorbike may not be overtaken on the inside of this line.
