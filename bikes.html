Track bicycles

About the track bicycle

If you're new to track cycling, you might think that a track bike isn't to different from any other bike, but you'd be wrong. A track bicycle differs from other bicycles in some very significant ways.

Fixed gear

Track bicycles use a so called fixed gear drivetrain. It means that as long as the wheels of the bike are spinning, so are the pedals. This is because fixed gear bikes lack a freewheel. A freewheel of some kind is present on almost all bikes, and allows the rider to "coast", or stop pedaling while the bike is still rolling. This is is impossible to do on a fixed gear bicycle, as the rear sprocket, is directly connected to the wheel. Fixed gear bikes also lack, well, gears. If you want to go faster, you simply have to pedal faster. This isn't much of a problem, since there are no hills in the velodrome (the track).

No Brakes

If you are familiar with what the cockpit of a road bike looks like, you'll see that the bike in the picture above lacks brake levers. It might seem like madness, but even though many track cyclist ride at speeds of 60 km/h or more, track bikes don't have any brakes. The only way to stop a track bike is to backpedal, but when you're moving at the speeds of an experienced track cyclist, going from full speed to standing still takes a very long time. There is also the fact that there isn't much of a need to be able to stop quickly, as focus is on going faster than your opponent.

Street use

Using track bikes on the street was originally popularized by bicycle messengers. Messengers appreciated the simplicity of the track bike, as it meant it wasn't Very prone to have mechanical issues. Having no brakes, track bikes were also less appealing to steal, meaning a messenger could feel reasonably safe leaving their bike unattended while picking up or dropping off a package. Nowadays, using fixed gear bikes on the street is quite popular. Some people equip their fixed gear bikes with breaks with flat handlebars and brakes, to make them better suited for street riding. These are sometimes refered to as tarck bikes, as a lighthearted way of mocking them for not being true track bicycles.
